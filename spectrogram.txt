      <p>
       A
       <b>
        spectrogram
       </b>
       is a visual representation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_density" title="Spectral density">
        spectrum
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">
        frequencies
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
        sound
       </a>
       or other signal as they vary with time. Spectrograms are sometimes called
       <b>
        spectral waterfalls
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        voiceprints
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        voicegrams
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spectrograms are used extensively in the fields of
       <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">
        music
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">
        sonar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_processing" title="Speech processing">
        speech processing
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismology
       </a>
       , and others. Spectrograms of audio can be used to identify spoken words
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetically
       </a>
       , and to analyse the
       <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">
        various calls of animals
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A spectrograph can be generated by an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_spectrometer" title="Optical spectrometer">
        optical spectrometer
       </a>
       , a bank of
       <a href="/wiki/Band-pass_filter" title="Band-pass filter">
        band-pass filters
       </a>
       or by
       <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">
        Fourier transform
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Format">
        Format
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectrogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Format">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A common format is a graph with two geometric dimensions: one axis represents
       <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">
        time
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RPM" title="RPM">
        RPM
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title='"No'>
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       the other axis is
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        frequency
       </a>
       ; a third dimension indicating the
       <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">
        amplitude
       </a>
       of a particular frequency at a particular time is represented by the
       <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">
        intensity
       </a>
       or color of each point in the image.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are many variations of format: sometimes the vertical and horizontal axes are switched, so time runs up and down; sometimes the amplitude is represented as the height of a 3D surface instead of color or intensity. The frequency and amplitude axes can be either
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">
        linear
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">
        logarithmic
       </a>
       , depending on what the graph is being used for. Audio would usually be represented with a logarithmic amplitude axis (probably in
       <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">
        decibels
       </a>
       , or dB), and frequency would be linear to emphasize harmonic relationships, or logarithmic to emphasize musical, tonal relationships.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">
        Generation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectrogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Generation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spectrograms of light may be created directly using an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_spectrometer" title="Optical spectrometer">
        optical spectrometer
       </a>
       over time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spectrograms may created from a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-domain" title="Time-domain">
        time-domain
       </a>
       signal in one of two ways: approximated as a filterbank that results from a series of
       <a href="/wiki/Band-pass_filter" title="Band-pass filter">
        band-pass filters
       </a>
       (this was the only way before the advent of modern digital signal processing), or calculated from the time signal using the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">
        Fourier transform
       </a>
       . These two methods actually form two different
       <a href="/wiki/Time%E2%80%93frequency_representation" title="Time–frequency representation">
        time–frequency representations
       </a>
       , but are equivalent under some conditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The bandpass filters method usually uses
       <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">
        analog
       </a>
       processing to divide the input signal into frequency bands; the magnitude of each filter's output controls a transducer that records the spectrogram as an image on paper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Creating a spectrogram using the FFT is a
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">
        digital process
       </a>
       . Digitally
       <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(signal_processing)" title="Sampling (signal processing)">
        sampled
       </a>
       data, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">
        time domain
       </a>
       , is broken up into chunks, which usually overlap, and Fourier transformed to calculate the magnitude of the frequency spectrum for each chunk. Each chunk then corresponds to a vertical line in the image; a measurement of magnitude versus frequency for a specific moment in time (the midpoint of the chunk). These spectrums or time plots are then "laid side by side" to form the image or a three-dimensional surface,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or slightly overlapped in various ways, i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Window_function#Overlapping_windows" title="Window function">
        windowing
       </a>
       . This process essentially corresponds to computing the squared
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">
        magnitude
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Short-time_Fourier_transform" title="Short-time Fourier transform">
        short-time Fourier transform
       </a>
       (STFT) of the signal

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
        Applications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectrogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Early analog spectrograms were applied to a wide range of areas including the study of bird calls (such as that of the
        <a href="/wiki/Great_tit" title="Great tit">
         great tit
        </a>
        ), with current research continuing using modern digital equipment
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and applied to all animal sounds. Contemporary use of the digital spectrogram is especially useful for studying
        <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">
         frequency modulation
        </a>
        (FM) in animal calls. Specifically, the distinguishing characteristics of FM chirps, broadband clicks, and social harmonizing are most easily visualized with the spectrogram.
       </li>
       <li>
        Spectrograms are useful in assisting in overcoming speech defects and in speech training for the portion of the population that is profoundly
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_impairment" title="Hearing impairment">
         deaf
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The studies of
        <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
         phonetics
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Speech_synthesis" title="Speech synthesis">
         speech synthesis
        </a>
        are often facilitated through the use of spectrograms.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        By reversing the process of producing a spectrogram, it is possible to create a signal whose spectrogram is an arbitrary image. This technique can be used to hide a picture in a piece of audio and has been employed by several
        <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">
         electronic music
        </a>
        artists.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        See also
        <a href="/wiki/Steganography" title="Steganography">
         steganography
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Some modern music is created using spectrograms as an intermediate medium; changing the intensity of different frequencies over time, or even creating new ones, by drawing them and then inverse transforming. See
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_timescale-pitch_modification" title="Audio timescale-pitch modification">
         Audio timescale-pitch modification
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Phase_vocoder" title="Phase vocoder">
         Phase vocoder
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Spectrograms can be used to analyze the results of passing a test signal through a signal processor such as a filter in order to check its performance.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        High definition spectrograms are used in the development of RF and microwave systems
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Spectrograms are now used to display
        <a href="/wiki/Scattering_parameters" title="Scattering parameters">
         scattering parameters
        </a>
        measured with vector network analyzers
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
         US Geological Survey
        </a>
        now provides real-time spectrogram displays from seismic stations
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_and_resynthesis">
        Limitations and resynthesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectrogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Limitations and resynthesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       From the formula above, it appears that a spectrogram contains no information about the exact, or even approximate,
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">
        phase
       </a>
       of the signal that it represents. For this reason, it is not possible to reverse the process and generate a copy of the original signal from a spectrogram, though in situations where the exact initial phase is unimportant it may be possible to generate a useful approximation of the original signal. The Analysis &amp; Resynthesis Sound Spectrograph
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is an example of a computer program that attempts to do this. The
       <a href="/wiki/Pattern_playback" title="Pattern playback">
        Pattern Playback
       </a>
       was an early speech synthesizer, designed at
       <a href="/wiki/Haskins_Laboratories" title="Haskins Laboratories">
        Haskins Laboratories
       </a>
       in the late 1940s, that converted pictures of the acoustic patterns of speech (spectrograms) back into sound.
      </p>
      <p>
       In fact, there is some phase information in the spectrogram, but it appears in another form, as time delay (or group delay) which is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_(mathematics)" title="Dual (mathematics)">
        dual
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instantaneous_frequency#Instantaneous_frequency" title="Instantaneous frequency">
        Instantaneous Frequency
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The size and shape of the analysis window can be varied. A smaller (shorter) window will produce more accurate results in timing, at the expense of precision of frequency representation. A larger (longer) window will provide a more precise frequency representation, at the expense of precision in timing representation. This is an instance of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg uncertainty principle">
        Heisenberg uncertainty principle
       </a>
       , that precision in two
       <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_variables" title="Conjugate variables">
        conjugate variables
       </a>
       are inversely proportional to each other.
      </p>
